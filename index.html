Misinformation and Polarization in the age of AI




NEWS: Find videos of each talk in the schedule!

Dec 14th, NeurIPS2024, Vancouver,

Overview

Spreading misinformation and increasing political and social polarization has been one of the major critiques of internet platforms. The vast reach of these platforms, combined with their dynamic of rewarding users for engagement and popularity, often at the cost of veracity and moderation, have propelled this to a seminal socio-technical challenge. The rise of generative AI has further exacerbated ramifications. Naturally, a nascent area of research aimed at modeling, analyzing, and addressing various aspects of misinformation and polarization on the internet has emerged. The approaches here are interdisciplinary, with works from computer science, cognitive science, economics, and law informing the literature herein. This workshop aims to bring together researchers from these areas to discuss the present challenges and mitigation strategies while fostering a robust academic community to tackle future developments.

Call for Contributions

We are soliciting contributions that address one of the below questions, or secondarily, another question on the intersection of modern machine learning and games. This year we are particularly interested in accepting work that uses non-standard formulations and applications for games in ML.

  • What are the individual incentives/psychology that lead users to spread misinformation or become polarized within online communities? [7, 5, 13, 3])
  • How can we structurally model (individual or aggregate) misinformation and polarization dynamics within internet platforms? [12]
  • Can softer approaches involving platform design limit the reach and spread of misinformation or polarization? [10, 9]
  • How can we model the effectiveness of stricter approaches like content moderation on addressing these issues? [6]
  • Can machine learning improve the outcomes of these approaches and help them scale? [4]
  • What regulatory or policy frameworks can governments adopt to incentivize internet platforms to tackle these issues? [8]
  • What are the challenges and promises posed by large language models and generative AI with respect to these issues? [11, 2]

Organizers

Safwan Hossain (Harvard University)

Safwan Hossain is a PhD candidate at Harvard University, with research interests broadly at the intersection of economics and computer science. Specifically, he is interested in questions of strategic behaviour, information design, fairness, and incentives that arise in supervised or online learning settings with multiple agents. He has published as a first author at venues including ICML, NeurIPS, WebConf, and EC.

Andjela Mladenovic (Mila & University of Montreal)

Andjela Mladenovic is a PhD candidate at Mila and DIRO at the University of Montreal. Her research interest includes problems at the intersection of game theory and machine learning, with an emphasis on cooperation within multi-agent AI systems. She has published work at venues including ICML, ICLR and WebConf. She is also a recipient of the FRQNT and Mila EDI Fellowship.

Yiling Chen (Harvard University)

Yiling Chen is a Gordon McKay Professor of Computer Science at Harvard University. Her research lies in the intersection of computer science and economics, with a focus on the social aspects of computational systems. She was selected by IEEE Intelligent Systems as one of “AI’s 10 to Watch” early in her career. Her research received best paper awards at ACM EC, AAMAS, ACM FAT* (now ACM FAccT) and ACM CSCW conferences. She was a program co-chair for the 2013 Conference on Web and Internet Economics (WINE’13), the 2016 ACM EC (EC’16), the 2018 AAAI Conference on Human Computation and Crowdsourcing (HCOMP’18) and the 2023 AAAI Conference on Artificial Intelligence (AAAI-23).

Gauthier Gidel (Mila & University of Montreal)

Gauthier Gidel is an assistant professor at Mila and DIRO at the University of Montreal, and Canada CIFAR AI chair holder. His work is at the intersection of game theory, machine learning, and optimization; he is well known for his work on differentiable game optimization. He co-organized the NeurIPS 2018 and Neurips 2019 workshops on Smooth Games Optimization and Machine Learning, the Montreal AI Symposium in 2021 as a program chair and a member of the ICLR organizing committee from 2022 to 2024 as a BlogPost track chair.

Aaron Courville

Aaron Courville is a professor at Mila and DIRO at the University of Montreal, a Canada CIFAR AI Chair, and a CIFAR Fellow of the Learning in Machines and Brains program. He has made key contributions to the development of Deep Learning, including co-authoring the seminal “Generative Adversarial Network” paper and the primary textbook on deep learning. His current research interests include computer vision, natural language and vision-language models, reasoning, reinforcement learning, and multi-agent reinforcement learning. He has organized several workshops in the past, including ones at ICML 2019, ICLR 2020, and NeurIPS 2020.
